# Event-Card Flavor Texts

*IMPERIUM: Twilight of Empires* — flavor only. Mechanical effects live in `docs/EVENT_CARDS.md`.
Each slug is stable and unique; join on the heading.

## Era I — rounds 1–5 (minor)

### council-of-florence
The union is signed at Florence, and the ink is scarcely dry upon the parchment. In Constantinople the bells ring for concord, while in the streets men already mutter that a peace sealed with Rome is a sold soul. Faith, it seems, is easier to sign than to keep.

### genoese-venetian-war
Two republics that share a sea cannot share a market. Their galleys trade broadsides from the Golden Horn to the Aegean, and every sunk hull is a rival's ledger set right. The Levant watches, and quietly raises its prices.

### walls-earthquake
The ground shrugs, and a span of the Theodosian walls comes down in dust and old mortar. The masons are summoned before the rubble settles, for a breach unmended is an invitation written in stone. The treasury groans louder than the earth.

### mercenary-revolt
Unpaid swords are the sharpest of all. The hired companies grumble in their camps, then grumble in the streets, and a captain's promise is worth only the coin behind it. Better to open the purse than to feel its emptiness at the gate.

### good-harvest
The granaries fill, the barges ride low on the rivers, and for once bread is cheaper than complaint. A fat autumn buys a loyalty no sermon can. Let the lords feast while the sun allows it.

### plague-in-the-ports
A ship comes in on a fair wind and a foul hold. Within a week the harbor quarter buries more than it lands, and the merchants bar their doors against their own custom. Trade sickens with the sailors.

### silk-road-caravan
Out of the East comes the long train of camels, bearing silk and pepper and rumor in equal measure. The caravan-masters pay their tolls with a smile and keep the better half hidden in the saddlebags. Where the road runs, the treasury swells.

### papal-envoy
The Holy Father's legate arrives in scarlet, with a blessing in one hand and a bill in the other. He speaks of crusade, of union, of the debts owed to Saint Peter. Rome gives nothing that Rome does not expect returned.

### janissary-recruitment
The devshirme takes its due from the Christian villages of Rumelia: the strongest boys, marched away to become the Sultan's own. Raised to the faith and the drum, they will forget the mothers who wept and remember only the Porte. From sorrow the Sublime Porte forges its finest steel.

### hexamilion-wall
Across the neck of the Morea the old rampart rises again, six miles of stone against the tide from the north. The Despot counts it his shield; wiser men count the guns that have not yet been cast. A wall stands only as tall as the age that assaults it.

### guild-charter
The masters set their seal to a new charter, and the crafts of the city bind themselves in privilege and price. Apprentices grumble, merchants calculate, and the treasury takes its cut of every sanctioned trade. Order, once purchased, repays its price with interest.

### pilgrim-season
The roads to the holy places fill with the devout, staff in hand and coin in purse. Inns overflow, relics are kissed, and the pious depart lighter of pocket and heavier of grace. Faith travels well, and spends freely.

### venetian-galley-launched
In the Arsenal the shipwrights lay a keel at dawn and step a mast by dusk, and the Republic gains another arm upon the sea. The Lion of Saint Mark looks out from a new prow. Venice does not pray for empire; she builds it, plank by plank.

### akce-debasement
The mint shaves a little silver from each coin and calls the lighter akçe by the old heavy name. The soldiers are paid in full and cheated all at once. A treasury mended with base metal cracks in the cold.

### salt-tax-riot
A new levy on salt turns the market square to a shouting-place, then to a battlefield of overturned carts. The people will bear hunger before they bear a tax on the very thing that keeps hunger at bay. A prudent lord learns which coins are worth the collecting.

### monastery-tithe
The great houses of the monks hold granaries, vineyards, and the ear of Heaven. When the crown asks its tithe, the abbots weigh loyalty against the ledger and pray for guidance that favors the ledger. Piety, too, keeps accounts.

### condottiere-contract
A captain of fortune offers his company to the highest bidder, and his loyalty expires with his pay. His lances are sharp, his conscience negotiable, and his terms are read twice before signing. Buy the sword, but never trust the scabbard.

### papal-indulgence
Rome has set a tariff on heaven, and the pardoners go forth with seals, ledgers, and a fine gift for arithmetic. The faithful buy remission of sins and count it coin well spent. Grace was never free; now at least it has a price.

### imperial-coronation
In one land a crown descends; in another a sword is girded on; in both, the trumpets tell a story every man present agrees to believe. Largesse rains on the crowd, and the levies stand a little straighter for a lord newly anointed. Legitimacy burns brightest on the day it is lit.

### comet-omen
A blazing star drags its tail across the heavens, and every camp from the Danube to the Maeander reads it by the light of its own fears. Soldiers pray, or desert, or sharpen their blades, according to what their captains say it means. The star itself says nothing; it has never needed to.

### ottoman-interregnum
Bayezid rode to Ankara and did not ride home, and his sons divide the inheritance in the only fashion brothers know: with knives. Provinces slip quietly away while the princes hunt one another across two continents. The house that devoured kingdoms pauses, for a season, to devour itself.

### discovery-of-alum
In the hills behind Phocaea the miners strike alum, the humble stone without which no dye in Europe will hold. Now every clothier from Bruges to Florence must bargain with whoever keeps Chios. Scarlet is a fine color; the coin it fetches is finer.

### marriage-alliance
A bride crosses the frontier with a dowry in her train and a treaty in her person. Two courts that traded insults in Lent trade toasts by Michaelmas, and the border lords grumble at the peace. A daughter well married holds more ground than a fortress, and costs less to garrison.

### corsair-raid
Galliots slip out of Tunis on the evening tide, and by morning some harbor town is counting what the sea has taken back. The corsairs burn what they cannot carry and are gone before the watch-fires are answered. The sea keeps no law but the swiftest hull.

### serbian-despotate-submits
The Despot has spent a long life learning the geometry of a small realm set between two great appetites. He kneels to the heavier neighbor, pays his tribute to the day, and keeps his fortresses in excellent repair. Small realms do not choose their masters; they choose the hour of their kneeling.

### ragusan-tribute
Ragusa keeps no great host and wants none; her walls are ledgers and her weapons are receipts. When a strong fleet stands off her harbor, the Republic sends out wine, silver, and congratulations, all handsomely worded. She has outlasted a dozen conquerors this way and expects to outlast a dozen more.

### plague-of-locusts
The cloud comes out of the south with a sound like distant rain, and where it settles the fields go from green to stubble between one noon and the next. Priest and imam curse it, each in his own tongue, and the swarm acknowledges neither. What it leaves behind would not feed the men who prayed against it.

### fall-of-a-beylik
Another of Anatolia's little emirates tears itself apart over a dead emir's chair, and its garrison drifts off to feud, farm, or fortune elsewhere. The gates stand open behind a finished dynasty. The great powers circle such carcasses politely, each waiting to see who dares feed first.

## Era II — rounds 6–10 (crises)

### varna-crusade
The cross is raised across Hungary and beyond, and a Christian host gathers to hurl the Ottoman back over the straits. Legates preach, kings pledge, and the Danube fills with banners bound south. Hope marches; behind it, always, marches the reckoning.

### battle-of-varna
On the Black Sea shore the crusade meets the Sultan's line and breaks upon it. The young king of Poland and Hungary falls, his head raised upon a lance, and Christendom's last great sally into the East dies with him. The road to the City lies open now, and everyone upon it knows where the road ends.

### long-campaign
Hunyadi drives deep into the Balkans through the autumn mud, and for a season the crescent gives ground. Towns change hands, hostages change masters, and winter changes everyone's mind. Glory is gathered in the field and spent, always, on the way home.

### treaty-of-szeged
Envoys swear peace over the Gospel and the Koran alike, and the ink is scarcely dry before the legates urge the oath be broken. A vow sworn to an infidel, they whisper, is no vow at all. Men will remember, at Varna, what such wisdom purchased.

### second-kosovo
On the old field of ravens the armies meet again, and again the Christian host is ground to ruin across three days of slaughter. Hunyadi escapes with his life and little else. The plain has drunk this vintage before, and finds it familiar.

### fall-of-thessalonica
The second city of the Greeks, sold to Venice and stormed by the Turk, passes at last behind the crescent. Its people are led out in chains beneath their own walls, and the churches fall silent one by one. When Thessalonica falls, the wise begin to count the days of the City.

### black-death-returns
The pestilence comes again, as it always comes, riding the trade lanes it helped to make. The bells toll without pause until the ringers themselves take to their beds. Empires may contend for the world; the plague contends for the emperors.

### union-riots
The union with Rome is proclaimed in Hagia Sophia, and the City answers with fury rather than amen. Better the Sultan's turban than the Latin mitre, cry the mobs, not yet knowing how the choice will be granted. A faith divided cannot hold a wall.

### despotate-of-morea
In the southern Morea the imperial brothers hold their little courts, quarreling over a peninsula while an empire burns. They dream of restoration and practice only division. The last light of Byzantium flickers in Mistra, and squabbles over its own oil.

### hussite-mercenaries
Out of Bohemia come the war-wagons of the Hussites, their handgunners and their grim discipline for hire. They fought their own crusade to a standstill and now sell that hard schooling to any purse. New war rolls in on old wheels.

### venetian-monopoly
The Republic tightens her grip on the sea-lanes, and every cargo that crosses her water pays her toll. Rivals find their harbors quietly closed and their captains quietly bought. Venice fights her wars in the ledger, and wins them there.

### bank-of-saint-george
In Genoa the Casa di San Giorgio holds the debts of the Republic and, through them, the Republic itself. It funds fleets, farms taxes, and governs colonies from a counting-house. Where the state falters, the bank rules.

### timur-shadow
Word comes from the East of thrones toppled and cities heaped into towers of skulls. Once the great lame conqueror broke the Sultan himself at Ankara and bought Byzantium fifty years of borrowed breath. The East remembers what the whirlwind costs, and dreads its like again.

### corn-blockade
The grain fleets are turned back at the straits, and the price of bread climbs faster than any wall. A city may laugh at an army for a season, but never at an empty granary. Hunger is the patient besieger, and it never lifts the siege.

### famine-winter
The snows come early and stay late, and the granaries that seemed full in autumn stand echoing by Lent. Lords who hoarded grain find themselves rich in a coin no one can eat. A hungry province is a province half-lost already.

### relics-of-the-saints
A holy relic is borne through the streets in solemn procession, and the faithful press close for a touch of the sacred. Courage flows where the cynic sees only bone and gilt. In desperate years, a saint's finger is worth a company of spears.

### spies-in-the-camp
A cipher is intercepted, a servant talks too freely, and it grows plain that the enemy dines upon one's own counsels. Trust curdles; every friend is weighed and found wanting. War is fought first in the dark, long before the banners meet.

### janissary-discontent
In the barrack yards of Adrianople the kettles lie overturned, and the Sultan's own slaves name their price. They were taken as boys and forged into the finest infantry under heaven; now the blade turns in the hand that holds it. An army paid in promises soon collects in another coin.

### wallachian-revolt
Beyond the Danube the Voivode burns the tribute rolls and calls the country to horse. The marches were never truly held, only rented, and the rent has come due in iron. A yoke sits lightly on Wallachia until the day it does not sit at all.

### grain-fleet-lost
The convoy was sighted off Tenedos, riding low in the water and heavy with the harvest; it was never sighted again. Whether the sea took it or the corsairs did, the bakers cannot say, and the empty ovens do not care. Bread on the seabed feeds only the fishes.

### fire-of-the-arsenal
It began among the pitch barrels after the evening bell, and by midnight the shipyards were a second sunset. Half-built galleys burned upon their slipways like offerings, and the seasoned timber of twenty years was gone before the dawn. An empire of the sea is built in an arsenal, and there it can also die.

### papal-interdict
The bull arrives sealed in lead, and behind it the churches close their doors one by one. No mass, no marriage, no burial in consecrated ground, until the prince who shed Christian blood repents of it. Rome has no hosts to send, and needs none; the silence of the bells is army enough.

### schism
Two men now wear the fisherman's ring, and each hurls anathema at the other across the Alps. Princes shop for absolution as merchants shop for grain, and the price of heaven falls by the day. In the East the crescent watches, and marvels that the cross should quarrel with itself.

### mamluk-embargo
In Cairo the Sultan of Egypt closes his fist, and the spice road closes with it. Pepper sits under seal in the warehouses of Alexandria while the factors of Venice and Genoa bid against one another for the leavings, and Mamluk sails stand off Cyprus to see the lesson taught. He who holds the source of a river may name his own price for the water.

### anatolian-alliance
From Konya the emir of Karaman sends riders to every beylik that still remembers being free, and the old banners gather in the uplands. The ancient houses of Anatolia knew the sons of Osman when they were the least among them, and they mean to offer the reminder in steel. Empires learn late that the ground behind them was never conquered, only quiet.

### knights-of-rhodes-sortie
Out of Rhodes come the galleys of Saint John, black-hulled and white-crossed, crewed by monks who swore their vows with swords upon the altar. The corsairs scatter before them as gulls before a hawk. The old crusade has shrunk to one island and a handful of ships, yet the sea still keeps its manners where they pass.

## Era III — rounds 11–16 (existential)

### rumeli-hisari
On the European shore, at the narrowest of the straits, the Sultan raises a fortress in a single summer. Its guns can close the Bosporus to any hull that will not pay. They call it Boğazkesen, the Throat-Cutter, and the City understands the name.

### great-bombard-forged
The founder Orban, spurned by the Greeks, casts for the Sultan a bombard so vast it must be dragged by sixty oxen. Its stone shot flies farther than any wall was built to withstand. The age of proud stone ramparts ends in the roar of its first firing.

### chain-across-the-horn
A great boom of iron links is stretched across the mouth of the Golden Horn, and the inner harbor is sealed against the enemy fleet. Behind it the defenders' galleys ride safe, for now. A chain is a fine wall until the sea itself is outflanked.

### ships-over-land
In a single night the Sultan's men grease a road of timber over the hill and haul their galleys overland into the Horn. Dawn shows the defenders an enemy fleet where no fleet could sail. The chain still holds; it now guards nothing.

### final-siege
The full weight of the Ottoman host closes upon the City, camp upon camp from the Marmara to the Horn. The bombards speak by day and the sappers by night, and the walls are mended by torchlight with the very stones the guns threw down. There will be no lifting of this siege but one.

### giustiniani-wounded
Giovanni Giustiniani, the Genoese who held the breach as though it were his own city, is struck down at the crisis of the assault. Borne from the wall, he carries the defenders' heart away with him. When the captain leaves the line, the line remembers that it is only men.

### relief-fleet-fails
The eyes of the City turn west, to the sea, waiting for the sails that were promised. Day upon day the horizon stays empty, and the promises curdle into rumor. Christendom, it seems, will send its prayers and keep its ships.

### kerkoporta
In the tumult a small sally-port, the Kerkoporta, is left unbarred, and through that oversight the enemy finds the inner wall. So great a City, and it turns upon a single forgotten latch. The chroniclers will call it fate; it was a bolt not drawn.

### omen-in-the-sky
A strange light plays about the dome of Hagia Sophia and is gone; the moon rises in eclipse though the night is clear. The people read the heavens and find no comfort written there. When men look up for signs, they have already stopped looking for help below.

### hagia-sophia-vigil
On the last night the divided city gathers as one beneath the great dome — Greek and Latin, monk and soldier — and sings the old liturgy together at last. The union that the riots could not make, the ending makes freely. They pray as men pray who know the morning.

### no-relief-from-the-west
The councils of Rome and the courts of the kings debate the fate of the City in measured tones, and adjourn. Aid is voted, delayed, reconsidered, and never sent. The West will mourn Constantinople more warmly than ever it defended her.

### last-emperor
Constantine, eleventh of the name, puts off the marks of the purple and takes his place in the breach as a common soldier. He asks no man to die where he will not stand. An empire of a thousand years comes down to one man's word: that the City is worth the dying.

### desertion-in-the-night
Under cover of dark, boats slip from the sea-walls and steal for open water, bearing men who have weighed the odds and chosen breath over duty. Who can wholly blame them; who can ever quite forgive them. A wall is held by those who stay.

### sack-of-the-city
The gates give, the cry goes up, and for three days the soldiery takes its promised due of the richest city in the world. Palaces are stripped, the people led away in ropes, and the old wealth of Byzantium scattered to the winds and the wagons. What ten centuries gathered, three days disperse.

### fall-of-constantinople
On the twenty-ninth of May the crescent rises over the Queen of Cities, and the Roman Empire, that had stood since Augustus, is no more. The Sultan rides to Hagia Sophia and prays where the emperors were crowned. An age closes with the great doors; the world, not yet knowing it, has become new.

### the-empty-treasury
The emperor melts the plate of the churches to pay his soldiers, and still the coffers ring hollow. A crown without coin commands only as far as loyalty reaches, and loyalty, unpaid, wears thin. The last defense of a city is often the first thing it can no longer afford.

### gunpowder-revolution
The bombards speak, and stone that stood a thousand years answers in dust. Every prince now buys saltpetre before marble, and the founders of great guns dine better than bishops. The age of the high wall is over; the walls simply have not heard yet.

### final-crusade
The last summons goes out from Rome: take the cross, or watch the City die from a safe distance. For once the appeal is more than rhetoric — hosts are gathering, galleys are fitting out, and the road east fills with men who mean it. The chronicle keeps two lists, those who marched and those who found reasons, and no prince should doubt which page his name goes on.

### drought
The rains do not come, and from Thrace to the Morea the rivers run thin and shameful between their banks. The herds of Anatolia stand grey with dust on cracked earth, and the granaries of the Balkans echo before the first snow. The sky withholds, and no siege engine ever built can be turned against it.

### financial-crisis
A rumor runs through the counting-houses of Genoa, and by evening the doors of the great banks are barred from within. Credit, that invisible empire, collapses in an afternoon, and debts written in fine Italian hands all come due at once. Gold buried in cellars proves no safer; in a fearful season, even a full strongbox pays its tithe.

### byzantine-civil-war
A Palaiologos of the second rank raises his standard and calls himself Basileus, and what is left of empire turns to fighting over the shroud. The Ottomans need not lift a scimitar; Byzantium has always been generous with its own blood. The claimant may yet be bought — a purse has settled worse quarrels, and cheaper thrones.

### peace-of-turin
At Turin the exhausted republics set down their swords, having discovered that neither can afford the other's ruin. The war galleys go home, the sea lanes open, and the counting-houses of the Rialto and the Ripa fill again. It was never the fleets that ended the war; it was the ledgers, which never lie and never tire.
